# Google Helpful Content Guidelines
**Source:** https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/creating-helpful-content

Use this reference during Phase 6 (E-E-A-T & Content Quality) to assess whether a page meets Google's people-first content standards.

---

## Content & Quality Self-Assessment

Ask these questions about the audited page:

- Does the content provide **original information, reporting, research, or analysis**?
- Does it provide a **substantial, complete, or comprehensive description** of the topic?
- Does it offer **insightful analysis or interesting information beyond the obvious**?
- If it draws on other sources, does it **avoid simply copying or rewriting** and instead add substantial original value?
- Does the **main heading/title** provide a descriptive, helpful summary — without exaggerating or being sensational?
- Is this the sort of page you'd **bookmark, share with a friend, or recommend**?
- Would you expect to see this content **referenced by a printed magazine, encyclopedia, or book**?
- Does the content have **spelling or stylistic issues**, or does it appear sloppy or hastily produced?
- Is the content **mass-produced** by many creators or spread across a large network of sites with little individual attention?

---

## Expertise Signals

- Does the content present information in a way that **makes you want to trust it** — clear sourcing, evidence of expertise, author background, About page?
- Would someone researching the site come away with an impression that it is **well-trusted or widely recognized as an authority**?
- Is the content **written or reviewed by an expert or enthusiast** who demonstrably knows the topic?
- Does the content have **easily-verified factual errors**?

---

## People-First Content Checklist

A page demonstrates people-first content when:

- [ ] There is an **existing or intended audience** that would find the content useful if they came directly to the site
- [ ] The content clearly demonstrates **first-hand expertise and depth of knowledge** (e.g., actually used the product, visited the place)
- [ ] The site has a **primary purpose or focus**
- [ ] After reading, someone will feel they've **learned enough to achieve their goal**
- [ ] The reader will feel they've had a **satisfying experience**

---

## Search Engine-First Content Warning Signs

Flag these as quality issues during the audit:

- Content that exists **primarily to attract search engine visits** (not for a real audience)
- **Mass content production** across many topics hoping some will rank
- **Extensive automation** to produce content on many topics
- **Summarizing what others say** without adding value
- Writing about topics **only because they're trending**, not because the site has genuine expertise
- Content that leaves readers **needing to search again** for better information
- Adding word count **not because it serves the reader**, but chasing a perceived Google preference
- **Changing dates on pages** to appear fresh when content hasn't substantially changed
- Entering a niche **without real expertise**, motivated only by search traffic
- Content that **promises to answer questions** that have no confirmed answer (fake release dates, speculation presented as fact)

---

## E-E-A-T: Who, How, Why Framework

Google asks three questions about every piece of content:

### Who Created It?
- Is it **self-evident who authored** the content?
- Do pages carry a **byline** where one is expected?
- Do bylines **link to an author page** with background on their expertise?

### How Was It Created?
- If automation or AI was used, is this **disclosed**?
- Is there **context about how AI/automation was used** and why it was seen as useful?
- Transparency about production methods builds reader trust.

### Why Was It Created?
This is the **most important question**. The purpose should be helping people.

Red flags:
- Content created **primarily to attract search engine visits**
- Using automation/AI **primarily to manipulate search rankings** (violates spam policies)

---

## YMYL — Extra Scrutiny

Google gives extra weight to E-E-A-T for **Your Money or Your Life** topics that affect:
- Health and medical decisions
- Financial planning and investment
- Safety information
- Societal well-being

For YMYL pages, the absence of expert authorship, sourcing, or professional review is a **Critical** finding.

---

## Page Experience

Google's core ranking systems reward content that provides a **good page experience**. This is holistic — not isolated factors. Signals include:
- Core Web Vitals (LCP, INP, CLS)
- Mobile-friendliness
- HTTPS
- Absence of intrusive interstitials

---

## SEO & Helpful Content — Not in Conflict

> "SEO can be a helpful activity when it is applied to people-first content, rather than search engine-first content."

Good SEO amplifies content that already deserves to rank. It does not replace the need for genuine expertise, original research, and a real audience.

---

## Audit Application

When running Phase 6, use this reference to answer:
1. **Would a knowledgeable human trust and recommend this page?**
2. **Does the content exist for users or for search engines?**
3. **Is authorship transparent and credible?**
4. **Does it satisfy the searcher's full intent, or would they bounce to find something better?**

Flag any "No" answers as content quality issues with the severity matching how central the gap is to the page's purpose.
